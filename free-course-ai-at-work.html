Lesson 3 of 7

The 5 Biggest Mistakes People Make with AI at Work

Here's the truth most AI content won't tell you: a lot of people are using AI and getting mediocre results — not because AI is bad, but because they're making these five critical mistakes.

40%
40% of time saved by AI is lost to fixing AI mistakes.

Workers average nearly 2 hours of rework per AI error. The people who benefit most from AI are the ones who know how to prompt well and review outputs critically.

  • 1

    Using the tool, skipping the strategy

    Most people open ChatGPT, ask something random, copy-paste it, and call it "using AI." That's not integration — that's chaos. AI works best when it's built into your workflow intentionally, not used as a last-minute assistant.

  • 2

    Giving terrible prompts

    "Write me an email about my business" is not a prompt. The output you get reflects the input you give. Vague prompt = vague result. The biggest gap between average and great AI users is almost always the prompt quality.

  • 3

    Using AI for complex judgment calls

    AI is exceptional at drafting, summarizing, structuring, and generating ideas. It should NOT be making nuanced decisions about your people, your strategy, or your ethics. Those require you — the human.

  • 4

    Inputting sensitive or confidential data

    Never paste proprietary client information, passwords, financial account details, or HIPAA-protected data into public AI tools. Use enterprise versions with proper security agreements if that's required for your work.

  • 5

    Switching tools instead of improving prompts

    "This AI tool is bad, let me try another one." 90% of the time, the tool isn't the problem — the prompt is. Master prompting in one tool before you start shopping for another.

📊 Research-Backed Reality

Researchers found that workers who use AI frequently (daily) experience "AI brain fry" — increased mental fatigue from constantly monitoring AI output for errors. The solution: use AI for low-stakes tasks, apply human judgment for high-stakes ones. That's the balance that leads to sustainable productivity gains.

Lesson 4 of 7

The 7 Best Practices of High-Performing AI Users

The top 20% of AI users — the ones capturing most of the productivity and revenue gains — consistently do these seven things. These aren't complicated. They just require intention.

  • ✅Start with the problem, not the tool.  Ask: "What takes me the most time that's also repetitive?" That's where AI gives you the highest return. Don't just open AI and hope for inspiration.
  • ✅Give AI context, format, and tone — every time.  The best prompts include: WHO the audience is, WHAT format you want, WHAT tone to use, and WHAT the goal is. All four = dramatically better output.
  • ✅Start small, measure results, then expand.  Pick one task. Use AI on it for two weeks. Measure the time saved. Then expand to another task. Don't try to automate everything at once.
  • ✅Build AI into your workflow, not on top of it.  The most effective AI users have specific prompts they run at specific times — "Every Monday I run this prompt." Not "I use AI when I remember to."
  • ✅Always review and edit AI output before using it.  AI is your first draft generator, not your final answer. Review, personalize, and add your voice. The goal is to cut time, not cut corners.
  • ✅Protect sensitive data — always.  If you wouldn't post it publicly on the internet, don't paste it into a public AI tool. Simple rule. Non-negotiable.
  • ✅Keep your prompts.  The prompts that work well are gold. Save them in a document, a notes app, anywhere. Don't reinvent the wheel every time.
The 4-Part Prompt Framework
Every great prompt has four parts:

[TASK] + [CONTEXT] + [FORMAT] + [TONE]

Example: "Write a follow-up email [TASK] to a prospect who attended my webinar last week but hasn't purchased yet — they run a small healthcare practice [CONTEXT]. Under 150 words, 3 paragraphs: acknowledge the delay, give one key benefit, and end with a soft CTA to book a call [FORMAT]. Warm, professional, not pushy [TONE]."
💡 This framework applies to literally every prompt you'll ever write. Learn it once. Use it forever.
